# Curriculum Book Analysis — Portuguese Language Learning

> Research extracted from three textbooks for curriculum design.
> Date: 2026-03-22

## Books Analyzed

1. **Portuguese For Dummies** — Karen Keller (Wiley, 2006) — Brazilian Portuguese focus
2. **Portuguese Phrases For Dummies** — Karen Keller (Wiley, 2007) — Brazilian Portuguese focus
3. **Portuguese in 3 Months** — Maria Fernanda Allen (DK/Hugo, 2022, 3rd ed.) — European Portuguese focus (with Brazilian variants noted throughout)

---

## 1. How the Books Structure the Learning Path

### Portuguese For Dummies (Brazilian)

Three-part progression:

**Part I — Getting Started (foundation):**
1. Pronunciation and the alphabet (consonants, vowels, accent marks, regional variation)
2. Basic grammar overview (nouns, adjectives, articles, pronouns, verb conjugation, contractions, indirect objects, numbers)
3. Greetings and introductions (ser vs. estar, falar, formal/informal)

**Part II — Portuguese in Action (situational survival):**
4. Small talk (nationalities, weather, family, possessives, question words, contact info)
5. Dining and markets (food vocab, ordering, eating/drinking verbs, querer)
6. Shopping (clothing, comparisons, typical Brazilian items, bargaining)
7. Beach (beachwear, beauty expressions, soccer, likes/dislikes)
8. Going out (invitations, time of day, days of week, music/dance/arts/cinema)
9. Telephone (phone calls, spelling, past tense introduction, connector words)
10. Office and home (work verbs: fazer/trabalhar, housing, email)

**Part III — Portuguese on the Go (travel and emergencies):**
11. Money and banks
12. Directions and navigation
13. Hotels
14. Transportation
15. Trip planning
16. Emergencies
17. Carnaval (cultural capstone)

**Part IV — Part of Tens (shortcuts and cultural tips):**
- 10 ways to get a handle on Brazilian Portuguese quickly
- 10 favorite Brazilian expressions
- 11 common slang words
- 11 terms that make you sound fluent

### Portuguese Phrases For Dummies (Brazilian)

Compact 13-chapter structure covering similar ground to the full Dummies book, but organized as a pocket reference:
1. Pronunciation
2. Grammar essentials (present/past/future tenses, contractions, commands, possessives)
3. Numbers, time, dates, money, measurements
4. Greetings, small talk, ser/estar, family, contact info
5. Food and restaurants
6. Shopping
7. Leisure (going out, beach, music, Carnaval, sports, romance)
8. Work and phone calls
9. Transportation
10. Accommodation
11. Emergencies
12–13. Ten favorite expressions + ten phrases that make you sound local

### Portuguese in 3 Months (European, with Brazilian notes)

Twelve-week grammar-forward progression:

| Week | Core Topics |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Pronunciation; nouns and gender; articles (o/a/os/as, um/uma); greetings and polite phrases; ter and haver; yes/no; questions; subject pronouns; registers of "you" |
| 2 | Ser and estar (to be — permanent vs. temporary); adjectives (agreement); prepositions de/em/a/por/para; directions vocab; titles of address |
| 3 | Regular present tense (-ar/-er/-ir verbs); question words (como/quando/onde/quem/porque/que/quanto); demonstratives (este/esse/aquele) |
| 4 | Possessives (meu/teu/seu/nosso/vosso); cardinal numbers 1–1,000,000; ordinal numbers; telling time; days/months/seasons |
| 5 | Forming feminine nouns; forming plurals (all patterns including -ão/-ões/-ães/-ãos); the preterite (simple past) |
| 6 | Imperfect tense (habitual/ongoing past); verbs for "to know" (saber/conhecer); verbs for "to play" (brincar/jogar/tocar); verbs for "to take" (tomar/levar/tirar); meals vocab; adverbs of place (aqui/ali/lá/aí) |
| 7 | More on personal pronouns (direct/indirect/reflexive); combined pronoun forms; useful verbs andar and ficar; imperative mood |
| 8 | Past participles; present perfect and past perfect; reflexive verbs; reciprocal forms; passive voice; impersonal verbs |
| 9 | Subjunctive (formation and use); "if" clauses |
| 10 | Personal infinitive; present participle (-ing form); uses of infinitive; por vs. para; special uses of definite article |
| 11 | Reading practice; letter writing |
| 12 | Useful expressions: verbs dar/deixar etc.; Portuguese equivalents of English idioms; topical vocabulary (travelling, doctor, hotel, restaurant, house, shopping, clothes, hairdresser, entertainment) |

## 2. Vocabulary and Topics at Beginner (A1) Level

All three books consistently introduce the following vocabulary clusters at the very beginning:

### Greetings and Courtesy
- Olá / Oi (hello)
- Bom dia / Boa tarde / Boa noite (good morning/afternoon/evening)
- Tudo bem? / Tudo bom? (how are you? / all good?)
- Obrigado/a (thank you)
- De nada / Não tem de quê (you're welcome)
- Por favor / Faz favor (please)
- Com licença / Desculpe (excuse me / sorry)
- Sim / Não (yes / no)
- Tchau / Até logo / Até amanhã / Adeus (goodbye phrases)

### Self-Introduction
- O meu nome é... / Eu me chamo... / Eu sou...
- Qual é seu nome? / Como se chama?
- De onde você é? / Sou de... / Sou [nationality]
- E o seu? (And yours?)

### Countries and Nationalities
- Estados Unidos / americano/a
- Inglaterra / inglês/inglesa
- Canada / canadense
- Alemanha / alemão/ã
- França / francês/francesa
- Brasil / brasileiro/a
- Portugal / português/portuguesa

### Family Members
- mãe / pai (mother/father)
- irmão / irmã (brother/sister)
- filho / filha (son/daughter)
- avô / avó (grandfather/grandmother)
- marido / mulher (husband/wife)
- primo / prima (cousin)
- tio / tia (uncle/aunt)

### Weather
- sol (sun) / chuva (rain) / nuvens (clouds)
- quente (hot) / frio (cold) / úmido (humid)
- calor (heat) / inverno (winter) / verão (summer)
- guarda-chuva (umbrella) / chover (to rain)

### Numbers
- 1–20 (um/uma through vinte)
- Tens to 100: trinta, quarenta, cinquenta, sessenta, setenta, oitenta, noventa, cem
- 200–1000: duzentos, trezentos... mil
- Ordinals: primeiro–décimo

### Time
- Que horas são? (What time is it?)
- São [number] horas. / É uma hora.
- e meia (half past) / e um quarto (quarter past) / menos vinte (twenty to)
- Days of week: segunda-feira through domingo
- Months: janeiro through dezembro

### Colors (introduced in shopping/clothes context)
- vermelho (red) / azul (blue) / verde (green) / amarelo (yellow)
- branco (white) / preto (black) / marrom/castanho (brown) / rosa (pink)

### Common Adjectives
- alto/baixo (tall/short) / grande/pequeno (big/small)
- bonito/feio (beautiful/ugly) / bom/mau (good/bad)
- caro/barato (expensive/cheap) / limpo/sujo (clean/dirty)
- simpático/antipático (nice/unfriendly) / interessante (interesting)
- velho/jovem (old/young) / cansado (tired) / feliz (happy)

### Food and Drink (introduced Week 1 in DK book; Chapter 5 in Dummies)
- café (coffee) / chá (tea) / água (water) / cerveja (beer) / vinho (wine)
- pão (bread) / arroz (rice) / feijão (beans) / frango (chicken)
- salada (salad) / sobremesa (dessert) / conta (the bill)
- Bom apetite! (enjoy your meal!)

### Essential Question Words
- O que? / Que? (what?)
- Onde? (where?)
- Quando? (when?)
- Como? (how?)
- Quem? (who?)
- Por que? / Porque (why? / because)
- Quanto/a? (how much?)
- Quantos/as? (how many?)

### Survival Phrases
- Não entendo. (I don't understand.)
- Pode repetir? (Can you repeat?)
- Fala mais devagar, por favor. (Speak more slowly, please.)
- Você fala inglês? (Do you speak English?)
- Onde fica...? (Where is...?)
- Quanto custa? (How much does it cost?)
- Me ajuda! (Help me!)

## 3. Grammar Introduction Order

### All Three Books Agree on This Core Sequence

**Step 1 — Pronunciation** (covered first in all three books before any grammar)
- Stress rules: normally falls on second-to-last syllable; exceptions with accents and word-final consonants
- Vowels: a/ã, e/ê, i, o/ô, u — including nasal sounds
- Key consonant sounds: c (k/s/ç), d (hard/j sound before i/e in Brazil), g (hard/zh), h (silent), j (zh), lh (ly), nh (ny), r (h/rolled), s (s/z), t (soft/ch), x (sh/ks)
- Diphthongs: ai, au, ao, ei, eu, oi, ou, ui
- Accent marks: acute (´), circumflex (^), tilde (~), grave (`)

**Step 2 — Nouns and Gender**
- All nouns are masculine or feminine
- Rules of thumb: -o endings → masculine; -a endings → feminine; -ção/-dade/-gem → feminine
- Definite articles: o / a / os / as
- Indefinite articles: um / uma / uns / umas
- Plural formation: add -s (vowel endings), -es (consonant endings), -m→-ns, -ão→-ões/-ães/-ãos

**Step 3 — Adjective Agreement**
- Adjectives agree in gender and number with the noun they describe
- Most follow -o/-a pattern; -e endings stay the same for both genders
- Adjectives usually follow the noun (opposite of English)
- Exception: some common adjectives always go before noun (bom, belo, mau, etc.)

**Step 4 — Subject Pronouns**
- eu, tu, você, o senhor/a senhora, ele/ela, nós, vocês, eles/elas
- Emphasis on the formal/informal distinction ("você" vs. "o senhor/a senhora")
- In Brazil: "a gente" often replaces "nós"; "cê" often replaces "você" in speech

**Step 5 — Two "To Be" Verbs: Ser and Estar** (universally the first major grammar lesson)
- **Ser** = permanent or lasting characteristics: identity, origin, profession, description
  - eu sou / você é / ele/ela é / nós somos / eles/elas são
- **Estar** = temporary states, location, feelings, progressive action
  - eu estou / você está / ele/ela está / nós estamos / eles/elas estão
- Ser de = origin/possession; Estar em = location
- In Brazil: estar + gerund (-ando/-endo); in Portugal: estar a + infinitive

**Step 6 — Present Tense Regular Verbs**
Three verb families based on infinitive ending:
- **-ar verbs** (most common): drop -ar, add -o/-a/-a/-amos/-am
  - falar, morar, trabalhar, gostar, dançar, comprar, estudar, falar
- **-er verbs**: drop -er, add -o/-e/-e/-emos/-em
  - comer, beber, viver, escrever, vender, aprender, compreender
- **-ir verbs**: drop -ir, add -o/-e/-e/-imos/-em
  - abrir, partir, assistir, dividir
- Key irregular verbs introduced early: ter (to have), haver (there is/are), ir (to go), querer (to want), fazer (to do/make), poder (can), saber (to know how), conhecer (to know/meet)

**Step 7 — Key Early Vocabulary Verbs**
Books consistently introduce these verbs as early priority:
- falar (to speak), morar/viver (to live), ter (to have), ser/estar (to be), ir (to go), querer (to want), gostar de (to like), fazer (to do/make), poder (can/to be able to), precisar de (to need), comer (to eat), beber (to drink), trabalhar (to work)

**Step 8 — Numbers, Time, Dates**
Introduced together in a dedicated section in all three books
- Cardinal numbers 1–100, then hundreds, thousands
- Ordinal numbers 1st–10th (at minimum)
- Telling time (Que horas são?)
- Days of week, months, seasons

**Step 9 — Prepositions and Contractions**
- de (of/from) + o/a/os/as = do/da/dos/das
- em (in/at/on) + o/a = no/na
- a (to/at) + o/a = ao/à
- por + o = pelo

**Step 10 — Question Words and Question Formation**
No auxiliary verb "do" — questions formed by intonation alone, or with question words
- que/o que, onde, quando, como, quem, por que, quanto

**Step 11 — Past Tenses**
- **Preterite** (simple past) introduced first: falar→falei/falou/falamos/falaram; for one-off completed actions
- **Imperfect** introduced second: falar→falava/falava/falávamos/falavam; for habitual/ongoing past

**Step 12 — Future Tense**
- Near future (ir + infinitive) introduced before formal future tense: vou falar, vai comer, vamos ir
- Formal future tense introduced later

**Step 13 — Advanced Grammar** (beyond A1, presented in Weeks 7–12 of DK book)
- Possessives; imperatives; object pronouns (direct/indirect/reflexive)
- Past participle; present perfect; reflexive verbs; passive voice
- Subjunctive; personal infinitive; present participle

## 4. Teaching Techniques and Exercise Types

### Portuguese For Dummies / Phrases For Dummies

**"Talkin' the Talk" dialogues:** Short, realistic conversations (4–10 lines) set in Brazil, showing the Portuguese, phonetic pronunciation, and English translation side by side. Each dialogue is tied to a specific situation (restaurant, hotel, beach, etc.). The companion CD lets learners hear native speakers.

**"Words to Know" blackboards:** After each dialogue, key vocabulary is collected in a visual "chalkboard" box with the Portuguese word, pronunciation, and English meaning. This reinforces high-frequency words in context.

**"Fun & Games" activities:** End-of-chapter exercises — fill-in-the-blank dialogues, matching exercises, word searches. These test the most basic vocabulary from the chapter, not edge cases.

**Pronunciation keys:** Every new word is presented with a phonetic guide in parentheses (stressed syllable in italics). This runs throughout every chapter, not just the pronunciation chapter.

**Cultural sidebars:** "Cultural Wisdom" boxes provide Brazilian cultural context alongside language — e.g., why Brazilians are informal with names, what a caipirinha is, how Carnaval works. These reinforce motivation to learn.

**"Part of Tens" rapid-fire lists:** Slang, idiomatic expressions, and fluency shortcuts are gathered at the end as digestible capsule lists — not buried in grammar chapters.

### Portuguese in 3 Months

**Concise grammar explanations:** Each grammar point is presented with a clear rule, immediate examples in both Portuguese and English, and "imitated pronunciation" phonetic guides for the first few weeks.

**Translation exercises (both directions):** Each section ends with 10–14 sentence translation exercises — half Portuguese-to-English, half English-to-Portuguese. This forces active recall, not passive recognition.

**Topical vocabulary boxes:** Each section has a vocabulary list in a box format (Portuguese + phonetic + English) tied directly to the exercises.

**Conversations:** Dialogue practice appears at the end of each week to reinforce vocabulary and idioms in natural speech. Students listen first, then read aloud, then imitate.

**Revision habit:** The preface explicitly instructs students to spend 10 minutes revising the previous day's material before starting anything new — spaced repetition built into the workflow.

**Cumulative structure:** Later weeks deliberately revisit and extend early grammar (e.g., plural rules in Week 1 are deepened in Week 5; adjective agreement introduced in Week 2 is extended in Weeks 5–7).

**"Imitated pronunciation" system:** A phonetic transcription system using English sound approximations, provided for the first few weeks, then gradually removed as learners internalize the sound system. This scaffolding-and-fade technique is explicit.

## 6. Key Curriculum Design Insights

### Shared Principles Across All Three Books

1. **Pronunciation comes first, always.** All three books open with pronunciation before a single grammar rule. This is non-negotiable — learners who skip it struggle with every subsequent lesson.

2. **Context drives vocabulary.** Vocabulary is never introduced as abstract lists. It always appears inside a situation (restaurant, hotel, directions, family talk). The situation gives meaning and memory hooks.

3. **Ser vs. estar is the first major grammar hurdle.** All three books treat this as the most critical conceptual challenge for English speakers and spend significant time on it with abundant examples.

4. **Regular -ar verbs are the gateway to conjugation.** The books consistently introduce -ar verbs first, build the pattern, then extend to -er and -ir. The pattern mastery with -ar makes -er/-ir feel incremental.

5. **Near future (ir + infinitive) before simple future.** "Vou fazer" is taught well before the formal future tense because it's far more common in spoken Brazilian Portuguese.

6. **The "you" problem needs early explicit attention.** The você / tu / senhor/senhora registers are confusing for English speakers and all books flag this immediately with clear guidance.

7. **Dialogues are the learning unit.** Every book uses realistic dialogues as the primary vehicle for presenting new language. Grammar is explained around dialogues, not separately from them.

8. **Cultural content is not optional.** The Dummies books especially weave Brazilian culture through every chapter. Language without cultural context loses half its meaning.

9. **The A1 survival kit (for a PWA app) should cover:**
   - Pronunciation rules (especially the tricky consonants c/d/g/h/j/lh/nh/r/t/x)
   - Greetings and introductions
   - Ser vs. estar with abundant practice
   - Present tense of the ~12 most important verbs
   - Numbers 1–100 + time-telling
   - Basic question words
   - Key situational vocabulary: food, family, directions, shopping

10. **Spaced repetition and active recall are the implicit methods.** The DK book explicitly builds daily review into its study plan. Both Dummies books put exercises at the end of every chapter. The pattern is: introduce → demonstrate in dialogue → extract vocabulary → drill with exercises → revisit later.

## European vs. Brazilian Portuguese Notes

The DK book (Portuguese in 3 Months) teaches European Portuguese but notes key Brazilian differences throughout:

| Feature | European | Brazilian |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Present continuous | estar a + infinitive (estou a trabalhar) | estar + gerund (estou trabalhando) |
| "to be hungry" | ter fome | ter fome (same) |
| "you" (informal) | tu (most common) | você (most common) |
| Final -l | strongly pronounced | faint/silent |
| d before e/i | soft (like English d) | j sound (jee/jah) |
| t before e/i | always t | ch sound (chee/chah) |
| Final r | pronounced | often silent |
| Bus stop | paragem | ponto de ônibus |
| Train | comboio | trem |
| Bathroom | casa de banho | banheiro |
| Girl | rapariga | moça |

For a Brazilian Portuguese-focused PWA, note these differences when sourcing pronunciation audio and selecting vocabulary.
